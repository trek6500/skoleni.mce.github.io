I welcome you to today's training, where we will focus on the quality of welds and the most common defects that may occur – spatter, porosity, and patches. Proper weld quality is crucial for the strength and durability of the structure, which is why it is important to be able to recognize these defects and respond to them correctly.

Before the welded structure reaches you, it has undergone a series of inspections and repairs, during which most of these defects were addressed. However, it can still happen that something escapes attention – and that is why it is important for you to be able to recognize potential defects in your work and inform the responsible quality person.

Today, we will explain how to identify these defects, what they may mean, and when it is necessary to report them to ensure that only high-quality and safe products reach the end customer.

1. Spatter

Spatter consists of small metal particles that adhere to the surface around the weld. It is usually removed during finishing, but if you find it, it is important to consider:

  • Mild spatter can be easily removed by grinding and is not a serious issue.
  • Excessive spatter that has not been removed can affect subsequent processes (painting, assembly).

For better understanding, take a look at materials number 1, 3, and 4, where you can see specific examples of spatter.

What to do? If you find significant spatter that has not been removed, inform the quality inspector.

2. Porosity

Porosity refers to small cavities on the surface or inside the weld that can weaken the strength of the joint. They are divided into pores on the ground surface and pores on the general surface.

Pores on the ground surface

These pores are visible only after the weld has been ground. You may notice them as:

  • Small cavities scattered across the surface.
  • Grouped deeper holes, which may indicate an internal defect in the weld.

These pores may be hidden beneath the surface, meaning the weld may look fine before grinding, but after removing the top layer, they will appear.

For a better understanding of this defect, take a look at material number 2, where you can see an example of pores that appeared after grinding the weld.

What to do?

  • If the pores are small and scattered, they can be tolerated. However, this is determined by the quality.
  • If they are deep and frequent, it is necessary to report it to the person responsible for quality.

Pores on the general surface

These pores are visible immediately after welding, without the need for grinding. You can recognize them by:

  • Visible small holes on the weld.
  • Accumulation of pores in a specific area of the weld.

These defects can be caused by poor gas protection, moisture in the material, or contamination.

3. Zápal

A burn is a place where the weld is not sufficiently fused or does not have a strong bond with the material. Such a defect can negatively affect the strength of the structure and lead to its failure.

How to recognize it?

  • Visible cracks, gaps, or a brittle weld.
  • Irregular weld shape or sharp edges..

Conclusion

Most of these defects are repaired before the product reaches you, but no inspection system is foolproof. If you encounter any of these defects during your work, it is not your fault, but it is important to inform the responsible person so they can ensure the repair is made.
